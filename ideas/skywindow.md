# SkyWindow

A few months ago I was hiking with a friend. On the way back, people 
had pulled over on the side of the road and were setting up 
telescopes. We stopped to see what was going on. One guy showed us 
a photo he'd just taken, Saturn, with the rings clearly visible, 
from a roadside in California, with a consumer telescope, on a 
Tuesday night.

I had no idea you could do that. I also had no idea how to find 
that again on my own.

That's the problem SkyWindow solves. Not for serious astronomers, 
they already have tools. For the person who saw something that made 
them curious and has no idea where to start.

The app does four things:

**Tonight's verdict** — based on your location, it tells you whether 
tonight is actually worth going outside. Cloud cover, moon phase, 
light pollution. Not a data dump, one clear answer with the reason.

**What you'll see** — specific objects visible tonight from your 
coordinates, and what they'll actually look like depending on whether 
you use your naked eye, binoculars, or a basic entry-level telescope. 
No jargon.

**Where to go** — the closest genuinely dark spots within driving 
distance. Sunnyvale has too much light pollution for a good view. 
But 20 minutes into the hills changes that significantly.

**Don't miss this** — saved alerts for upcoming events at your 
location. Meteor shower peaks, planetary alignments, ISS passing 
overhead. The app watches for these so you don't have to.

## Data
- NASA APoD API, free, provides daily astronomy imagery and 
  descriptions
- Open-Meteo API, cloud cover and visibility forecast, free, 
  no key needed
- wheretheiss.at API, real-time ISS location and pass predictions, 
  free
